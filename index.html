SCFHS Prometric Mock Exam

Full-Length Exam

1. Toddler is admitted to the pediatric room with several episodes of diarrhea 3 days. The child is diagnosed with gastroenteritis. Which organism is responsible for the most diarrhea episodes in children?

WHY: Rotavirus is the leading cause of severe diarrhea in young children.
HOW: Infection leads to watery diarrhea and dehydration, common in pediatric gastroenteritis.

2. A child with a diagnosis of Tetralogy of Fallot is scheduled for discharge from the hospital. The nurse should instruct caregivers that during a hyper cyanotic spell, which position is most likely to benefit the child?

WHY: Knee-chest position increases systemic vascular resistance.
HOW: This reduces right-to-left shunting and improves oxygenation during cyanotic spells.

3. A 12-year-old boy was brought to the Emergency due to respiratory arrest from drowning. Cardiac resuscitation is initiated. What is the major complication that might happen if treated quickly after drowning?

WHY: Hypoxia and anaerobic metabolism during drowning cause metabolic acidosis.
HOW: Prompt resuscitation may restore perfusion but acidosis must be monitored and corrected.

4. Which of the following is the leading cause of injury for children who are more than five years old?

WHY: Motor vehicle accidents are the most common cause of injury and death in school-aged children.
HOW: Safety measures, supervision, and restraint systems reduce risk.

5. A 1-year-old girl admitted with significant weight loss, diminished mid-arm circumference, diarrhea, and red hair. Which type of malnutrition does the nurse suspect?

WHY: Kwashiorkor is protein malnutrition, often with edema, hair changes, and diarrhea.
HOW: Red hair and wasted appearance indicate protein deficiency rather than caloric deficiency alone.

6. A 33-month-old infant is admitted to the Emergency Department with a fractured arm. The mother indicated that the child fell while crawling. Which observation would lead the nurse to suspect abuse?

WHY: Inconsistent explanations are a red flag for possible abuse.
HOW: Nurses must evaluate history and injury pattern to ensure child safety.

7. A 5-year-old child was brought to the Emergency Room with a fractured right forearm and several bruises. The child shows no signs of pain while palpating. He seems scared and unresponsive. Why should the nurse discuss this case with the nurse manager?

WHY: Multiple injuries with inconsistent history raise suspicion of abuse.
HOW: Escalating to management ensures proper investigation and child safety.

8. A 4-year-old child is seen in the Emergency Department with a spiral fracture of the left arm. X-ray shows previously healed broken bones. What is your immediate action?

WHY: Repeated fractures suggest abuse, requiring mandatory reporting.
HOW: Reporting ensures investigation and child protection.

9. A 62-year-old woman is admitted to the emergency department for the fourth time this year with severe injuries and bruises. What is your responsibility as a nurse to prevent such incidents?

WHY: Providing resources empowers the patient to prevent recurrence.
HOW: Safe shelter and support improve patient safety while complying with legal reporting.

10. A 5-year-old child admitted with a fractured arm shows bruises on the back. Each time the child was brought to the hospital, parents provided vague explanations. What is the pediatric nurse’s priority intervention?

WHY: Mandatory reporting ensures the child’s safety.
HOW: Suspected abuse must be escalated to authorities according to hospital protocol.

52. A nurse is assessing a patient with suspected pulmonary embolism. Which finding is most indicative?

WHY: Sudden dyspnea and pleuritic chest pain are classic PE signs.
HOW: Rapid assessment, oxygen, and notify physician for anticoagulation or imaging.

53. Which finding suggests early hypovolemic shock?

WHY: Early hypovolemic shock triggers compensatory tachycardia and mild hypotension.
HOW: Assess vitals, start IV fluids, and monitor perfusion.

54. A patient with diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) has K+ of 5.5 mmol/L. Which action is priority?

WHY: Fluid resuscitation is priority in DKA to restore perfusion.
HOW: Begin IV fluids, monitor potassium, and then start insulin when K+ is safe.

55. Which heart rhythm requires immediate defibrillation?

WHY: VF causes cardiac arrest and requires immediate defibrillation.
HOW: Call code, initiate CPR, and defibrillate per ACLS protocol.

56. A patient develops sudden confusion and slurred speech. Which is the first action?

WHY: Sudden neurological changes may be due to hypoglycemia.
HOW: Check glucose, treat low glucose immediately if confirmed.

57. Which finding indicates increased intracranial pressure in a child?

WHY: Headache, vomiting, and papilledema suggest raised ICP.
HOW: Monitor neurological status, elevate head, and notify physician.

58. Which lab is most critical to monitor in a patient receiving heparin?

WHY: aPTT measures effectiveness and safety of heparin therapy.
HOW: Adjust dose to therapeutic aPTT range and monitor for bleeding.

59. A patient in the ICU has a central line. Which action prevents catheter-related bloodstream infection?

WHY: Proper hand hygiene and aseptic technique reduce infection risk.
HOW: Follow central line bundle protocols consistently.

60. Which symptom is most suggestive of a myocardial infarction in an older adult?

WHY: Older adults may present atypically with fatigue, nausea, or dyspnea.
HOW: Prompt ECG, cardiac enzymes, and monitor closely.

61. A patient in septic shock is receiving norepinephrine. Which parameter best indicates adequate perfusion?

WHY: MAP ≥ 65 mmHg is the primary target for perfusion in septic shock.
HOW: Adjust vasopressors and fluids to maintain MAP while monitoring end-organ perfusion.

62. Which ventilator alarm indicates potential high airway pressure?

WHY: High-pressure alarms indicate obstruction, secretions, or decreased lung compliance.
HOW: Assess tubing, patient, and adjust settings to prevent barotrauma.

63. A patient with acute pancreatitis develops hypotension and tachycardia. What is the priority intervention?

WHY: Hypotension and tachycardia indicate hypovolemia in acute pancreatitis.
HOW: Rapid IV fluids restore perfusion and prevent shock.

64. Which finding suggests a tension pneumothorax in a ventilated patient?

WHY: Tension pneumothorax causes sudden hemodynamic compromise and unilateral absent breath sounds.
HOW: Immediate needle decompression followed by chest tube placement is required.

65. A patient with elevated intracranial pressure has a decreased level of consciousness. Which action is most important?

WHY: Mannitol reduces cerebral edema and ICP.
HOW: Administer per order, monitor fluid balance, and continue neuro checks.

66. Which electrolyte imbalance increases risk of digoxin toxicity?

WHY: Low potassium potentiates digoxin effects, increasing arrhythmia risk.
HOW: Monitor electrolytes and adjust medications accordingly.

67. A patient with ARDS is on mechanical ventilation. Which strategy prevents ventilator-induced lung injury?

WHY: Low tidal volumes reduce barotrauma and volutrauma in ARDS.
HOW: Use ARDSnet protocol, monitor plateau pressures, and adjust ventilator accordingly.

68. Which sign indicates hypovolemic shock in a burn patient?

WHY: Fluid loss from burns causes decreased circulating volume, leading to tachycardia and hypotension.
HOW: Rapid IV fluid replacement guided by burn formulas and ongoing assessment.

69. A patient develops anaphylaxis after a bee sting. Which intervention is first?

WHY: IM epinephrine is first-line for anaphylaxis to prevent cardiovascular collapse.
HOW: Administer immediately, maintain airway, and provide supportive care.

70. Which arterial blood gas indicates uncompensated metabolic acidosis?

WHY: Low pH and low HCO₃⁻ with normal PaCO₂ indicate uncompensated metabolic acidosis.
HOW: Identify cause (e.g., DKA, renal failure), correct underlying problem, and monitor electrolytes.

71. A post-operative patient develops sudden shortness of breath, chest pain, and hypotension. What is the most likely cause?

WHY: Sudden dyspnea, chest pain, and hypotension in a post-op patient strongly suggest pulmonary embolism.
HOW: Initiate anticoagulation per protocol and prepare for diagnostic confirmation.

72. Which action is most important when suctioning a tracheostomy patient?

WHY: Hyperoxygenation prevents hypoxemia during suctioning.
HOW: Administer 100% O₂ before and after brief suctioning periods (<10 sec each pass).

73. Which finding is an early sign of hypovolemic shock?

WHY: Tachycardia is a compensatory early sign before hypotension develops.
HOW: Monitor vitals closely and initiate fluid resuscitation promptly.

74. A patient with heart failure is prescribed furosemide. Which lab requires immediate monitoring?

WHY: Furosemide can cause hypokalemia, increasing arrhythmia risk.
HOW: Monitor K⁺ and replace if needed.

75. Which acid-base imbalance is most likely in a patient with prolonged vomiting?

WHY: Loss of gastric H⁺ ions from vomiting leads to metabolic alkalosis.
HOW: Monitor electrolytes, replace fluids, and treat underlying cause.

76. Which medication is priority for an acute asthma exacerbation in the ED?

WHY: SABA provides rapid bronchodilation in acute exacerbation.
HOW: Administer via nebulizer or MDI with spacer and monitor response.

77. Which intervention is most important for preventing ventilator-associated pneumonia?

WHY: Semi-recumbent position reduces aspiration risk.
HOW: Maintain HOB 30–45°, oral hygiene, and limit sedation when possible.

78. A patient with DKA has K⁺ 3.2 mEq/L. Which intervention is priority before insulin?

WHY: Insulin drives K⁺ into cells, worsening hypokalemia, risking cardiac arrest.
HOW: Replace potassium first, then start insulin and fluids.

79. Which is the earliest sign of increased intracranial pressure?

WHY: Headache and vomiting are early nonspecific signs; Cushing’s triad occurs later.
HOW: Perform neuro assessments, monitor ICP if indicated.

80. Which assessment indicates worsening shock in a patient with sepsis?

WHY: Low MAP and oliguria indicate inadequate perfusion and worsening shock.
HOW: Escalate fluids, vasopressors, and monitor organ function closely.

91. A patient presents with sudden severe abdominal pain radiating to the back, hypotension, and pulsatile abdominal mass. What is the most likely diagnosis?

WHY: Triad of hypotension, pulsatile mass, and severe back pain indicates AAA rupture.
HOW: Rapid surgical intervention is critical to survival.

92. Which electrolyte imbalance is most dangerous in a patient with chronic kidney disease on dialysis?

WHY: Hyperkalemia can cause life-threatening cardiac arrhythmias.
HOW: Monitor K⁺ closely, treat emergently with calcium gluconate, insulin, and dialysis if needed.

93. A patient with acute stroke suddenly develops severe headache, nausea, and vomiting after tPA therapy. What is the immediate concern?

WHY: tPA increases risk of intracranial bleeding, especially with sudden headache and nausea.
HOW: Stop tPA, perform urgent CT scan, and initiate neuro ICU management.

94. Which intervention is highest priority for a patient in anaphylactic shock?

WHY: Epinephrine is life-saving and reverses airway obstruction and hypotension.
HOW: Administer IM immediately, then provide oxygen and IV fluids as supportive therapy.

95. A patient has hypovolemic shock. Which finding indicates improving perfusion?

WHY: Adequate MAP and urine output indicate restored perfusion.
HOW: Continue monitoring and titrate fluids/pressors as needed.

96. A patient with pneumonia develops sudden hypotension, tachycardia, and cold extremities. Which type of shock is most likely?

WHY: Infection-induced hypotension with tachycardia and cool extremities indicates septic shock.
HOW: Start broad-spectrum antibiotics and fluid resuscitation immediately.

97. Which is the most important nursing action for a patient with suspected compartment syndrome?

WHY: Compartment syndrome is a surgical emergency to prevent permanent damage.
HOW: Notify surgeon, avoid elevation above heart, and monitor neurovascular status.

98. A patient with chronic liver disease develops confusion, asterixis, and elevated ammonia. Which treatment is priority?

WHY: Lactulose reduces ammonia absorption in hepatic encephalopathy.
HOW: Administer orally or rectally and monitor mental status.

99. Which intervention is priority in a patient with tension pneumothorax?

WHY: Tension pneumothorax is life-threatening and requires immediate decompression.
HOW: Perform needle thoracostomy, then insert chest tube.

100. A patient with myocardial infarction suddenly develops severe dyspnea, hypotension, and pulmonary edema. Which complication is most likely?

WHY: Acute MI can lead to left ventricular failure causing cardiogenic shock.
HOW: Provide oxygen, vasopressors, and consider mechanical support if indicated.
